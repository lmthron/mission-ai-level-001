Understanding AI Output - What to Trust and Verify

Review red flags, fact-checking strategies, and verification principles before taking your Module 3 checkpoint quiz. Critical thinking about AI output keeps you from trusting blindly.

The Core Problem
AI can be completely wrong while sounding completely right.

AI is incredibly good at sounding confident and authoritative. It presents information in clean, well-formatted text that looks professional. This creates a problem: AI can confidently state incorrect information. Remember from Module 1: AI predicts what words should come next based on patterns, not on what's actually true.

Six Red Flags - When AI Might Be Wrong

Overly Specific Without Sources

"Studies show that 73% of customers prefer..." - Where's that from?

Perfect Recall of Obscure Details

"In his 2018 interview with TechCrunch..." - Without a link, might be invented.

Exact Citations Without Links

"According to Journal of Applied Psychology, Vol. 45..." - Always verify.

Contradicts Itself

Says one thing in paragraph 2, opposite in paragraph 5.

Too Perfect of a Match

EXACTLY what you wanted with no nuance? Be suspicious. Real answers have caveats.

Note on Recent Events

Be cautious, though some AI tools can search for current information when needed.

Five Fact-Checking Strategies

Strategy 1: "Does This Exist?" Check

  1. Copy the exact claim
  2. Google it
  3. See if you find the actual source

If you find the report: ‚úÖ Verified. If you find nothing: ‚ùå Made up.

Strategy 2: "Multiple Sources" Check

Can you verify the claim in 2-3 other places? If sources disagree or you can't find confirmation, don't trust it.

Strategy 3: "Common Sense" Check

Does this make logical sense? Example: "Schedule all meetings for 15 minutes to reduce meeting time by 50%" - Technically possible but practically useless.

Strategy 4: "Ask It Again" Check

Try the same question in a new conversation. If AI gives wildly different answers, it's probably making things up.

Strategy 5: "Ask for Sources" Check

Ask: "Where did you get this information? Provide links." If AI says "based on my training data," you need to verify yourself.

What to Do When AI Is Wrong

Option 1: Refine Your Prompt

Often, bad output means unclear instructions. Example: "This is too generic. Give me specific examples relevant to payment processing companies."

Option 2: Start Over with More Context

Sometimes easier to start fresh with a better prompt than keep refining bad output.

Option 3: Break It Into Smaller Pieces

First: "List the key points I should cover." Review the list. Then: "Using these points, draft the email."

Option 4: Verify and Edit Yourself

Use AI as a starting point, not the final answer. Fact-check claims, add details AI doesn't know, adjust tone, fill gaps. You're the expert - AI is a tool.
