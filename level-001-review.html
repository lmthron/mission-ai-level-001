What AI Actually Is (and Isn't)

üéØ Foundation Concepts
Understanding Large Language Models (LLMs)

The Autocomplete Analogy

Think of an LLM like autocomplete on steroids. Your phone predicts the next word when texting - LLMs do that with billions of examples learned from massive amounts of text.

The Library Analogy

Imagine someone who has read every book in a massive library but doesn't actually understand the world. They can tell you what words usually come after other words, but they're not "thinking" like humans. They're pattern matching at an incredibly sophisticated level.

Key Insights

  • AI is very good at tasks involving language patterns (writing, summarizing, formatting, explaining)
  • AI doesn't "know" things the way you do (it can't fact-check itself or know what's true)
  • AI works best when you give it clear instructions and context
  • AI predicts likely responses based on patterns, not on truth
What AI Can Do Well
  • Summarize and synthesize: Process multiple sources into common themes
  • Draft and edit text: Create professional responses, adjust tone and style
  • Explain complex concepts: Break down technical topics into simple language
  • Generate options: Provide multiple alternatives and variations
  • Format and restructure: Transform notes into organized lists or tables
  • Answer based on provided information: Extract insights from documents you share
What AI Cannot Do
  • Replace human judgment: Doesn't know company policies, customer history, or appropriate context
  • Guarantee accuracy: Can confidently state wrong information (hallucinations)
  • Understand nuance perfectly: May miss sarcasm, cultural context, emotional weight
  • Make ethical decisions: Will do what you ask - you decide if it's appropriate
Common Misconceptions
‚ùå Myth

AI is thinking and understanding like a human

‚úì Reality

AI is predicting likely responses based on patterns in training data

‚ùå Myth

If AI says it confidently, it must be true

‚úì Reality

AI has no concept of truth. Generates plausible text regardless of accuracy

‚ùå Myth

AI will replace my job

‚úì Reality

AI is a tool for efficiency. Your judgment, context, and expertise remain essential

‚ùå Myth

I need to be technical to use AI

‚úì Reality

If you can type a question or request, you can use AI

üéØ Module 1 Key Takeaway:

AI is a powerful tool for language-based tasks, but it's pattern matching, not thinking. You're still in charge - AI assists, you verify and decide.

The Anatomy of a Good Prompt

üéØ Prompt Engineering
The Four-Element Framework

1. Context

What's the situation? Who's the audience? What's the purpose?

2. Specificity

Exactly what do you want? What format? What length?

3. Constraints

What should be included? What should be avoided?

4. Format

How should output be structured? Length, style, organization?

Common Prompting Mistakes
  • Asking for everything at once: Break complex requests into steps - first draft, then refine
  • Not providing examples: Show what "nicely" or "professional" means with concrete examples
  • Assuming AI knows your context: Be explicit - AI doesn't know what "this" is or what "fixed" means
  • Giving up after bad output: Refine your prompt and try again - bad output often means unclear instructions
Refining Through Iteration

AI rarely gets it perfect on the first try. Tell AI specifically what to fix:

  • "This is too formal. Make it more conversational and friendly."
  • "Add a section about next steps and who's responsible for what."
  • "Make this half as long and focus only on budget concerns, not timeline."
üéØ Module 2 Key Takeaway:

The difference between vague and specific prompts is massive. Use the four elements and iterate until you get what you need. AI is a tool you shape through feedback.

Understanding AI Output - What to Trust and Verify

üéØ Critical Evaluation
The Core Problem
AI can be completely wrong while sounding completely right.

AI is incredibly good at sounding confident and authoritative. This creates a problem: it can confidently state incorrect information. AI predicts what words should come next based on patterns, not on what's actually true.

Six Red Flags - When AI Might Be Wrong

Overly Specific Without Sources

"73% of customers prefer..." - Where's that from?

Perfect Recall of Obscure Details

Specific quotes or dates without verifiable links

Exact Citations Without Links

"According to Journal X, Vol. 45..." - Always verify

Contradicts Itself

Says one thing, then opposite later in response

Too Perfect of a Match

EXACTLY what you wanted with no nuance? Be suspicious

Note on Recent Events

Be cautious, though some AI tools can search for current info

Five Fact-Checking Strategies

1. "Does This Exist?" Check

Copy the exact claim ‚Üí Google it ‚Üí See if you find the actual source

2. "Multiple Sources" Check

Can you verify the claim in 2-3 other places? If sources disagree, don't trust it

3. "Common Sense" Check

Does this make logical sense or is it technically correct but practically useless?

4. "Ask It Again" Check

Try same question in new conversation. Wildly different answers? Probably making things up

5. "Ask for Sources" Check

Request links. If AI says "based on training data," verify yourself

High-Stakes vs. Low-Stakes

Low-Stakes (verify if convenient)

‚Ä¢ Brainstorming ideas
‚Ä¢ Casual colleague emails
‚Ä¢ Presentation structure
‚Ä¢ Project name options

High-Stakes (ALWAYS verify)

‚Ä¢ Facts shared externally
‚Ä¢ Legal/financial/medical info
‚Ä¢ Customer decisions
‚Ä¢ Official documents
‚Ä¢ Technical specs

The Rule:

The more important the output, the more you need to verify it.

üéØ Module 3 Key Takeaway:

AI can sound confident while being wrong. Always verify high-stakes information. Watch for red flags, use fact-checking strategies. Bad output often means a bad prompt - refine your request.

The Three-Question Decision Framework

1. Is it repetitive?

Yes ‚Üí Good for AI
No ‚Üí Maybe not worth setup

2. Would explaining take less time than doing?

Yes ‚Üí AI might help

Working with Files

Supported File Types

  • Images: Screenshots, photos, charts, diagrams
  • Documents: Word files, PowerPoint slides

How to Use Files

  • Upload a screenshot ‚Üí "What does this error mean?"
  • Upload a chart ‚Üí "Explain what this graph is showing"
